MSPB Appeals for Federal Employees

Contesting a removal, demotion, or long suspension before the Merit Systems Protection Board is a strict, deadline-driven process. Reizes Law represents federal employees nationwide from the initial filing through hearing and petition for review.

Who Can Appeal to the MSPB

The Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) is the independent adjudicator for most major adverse personnel actions taken against federal employees. Appeal rights are not automatic — they depend on your appointment type, tenure, and the nature of the action taken against you.

In general, the following employees may appeal certain adverse actions to the MSPB:

  • Non-probationary employees in the competitive service who have completed one year of current continuous service
  • Most excepted service employees who have completed two years of current continuous service in the same or similar positions (subject to statutory exceptions)
  • Career appointees in the Senior Executive Service facing reduction in pay, suspensions, or removals from the civil service (as opposed to removals from the SES into another position)
  • Preference-eligible veterans in certain excepted service positions

Appealable actions typically include removals, reductions in grade or pay, furloughs of 30 days or less, and suspensions of more than 14 days. Certain denials of within-grade increases, retirement decisions by OPM, and individual right of action (IRA) whistleblower claims also come before the Board.

The 30-Day Filing Deadline

An MSPB appeal must generally be filed within 30 calendar days of the effective date of the action, or 30 days after receipt of the agency's decision, whichever is later. This deadline is one of the strictest in federal employment law.

Late filings are only excused in narrow circumstances, and the burden to justify an extension rests with the employee. If you have received a final decision removing, demoting, or suspending you, treat every day that passes as a day lost.

Key Deadline

You generally have 30 calendar days from the effective date of the adverse action to file your MSPB appeal. Waiting to contact counsel can forfeit claims permanently.

What an MSPB Appeal Involves

An MSPB appeal is a trial-type administrative proceeding. Although more streamlined than federal court, it follows a defined sequence of steps with deadlines at each stage.

1. Initial Filing

The appeal is filed through the MSPB's e-Appeal Online system and assigns the case to a regional administrative judge. The filing identifies the agency action, the bases for challenge, and any affirmative defenses such as discrimination, retaliation, or harmful procedural error.

2. Acknowledgment Order and Discovery

The administrative judge issues an acknowledgment order setting deadlines and identifying issues. The parties then engage in limited discovery — interrogatories, document requests, and depositions — governed by MSPB regulations rather than the Federal Rules.

3. Prehearing Submissions

Before the hearing, the parties file prehearing submissions identifying witnesses, exhibits, and legal theories. The judge rules on objections, narrows the issues, and issues a prehearing order.

4. Oral Hearing

Hearings are typically held by videoconference. The agency bears the burden of proving its charges by a preponderance of the evidence (or substantial evidence in performance-based actions), and the employee may cross-examine witnesses and present their own case in chief.

5. Initial Decision

After the hearing, the administrative judge issues an initial decision. It becomes final 35 days later unless a party files a petition for review with the full Board.

6. Petition for Review

Either party may seek review by the three-member Board. From a final Board decision, further review is available in the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit (or, for mixed cases, a U.S. district court).

Possible Outcomes

MSPB litigation can end in several ways, and settlement is common at every stage. Typical outcomes include:

  • Reversal — the Board orders the agency to cancel the action, restore the employee, and pay back pay, benefits, and, where appropriate, attorney fees
  • Mitigation — the penalty is reduced (for example, from removal to a suspension) when the agency proves misconduct but the chosen penalty is unreasonable
  • Settlement — the parties resolve the case through a voluntary agreement, often involving resignation with a clean record, removal of documents from the personnel file, or a negotiated separation
  • Affirmance — the Board sustains the agency's action

Common Winning Arguments

Strong MSPB appeals are built on a careful review of the agency's evidence and the procedural record. Arguments that regularly move the needle include:

Douglas Factors Analysis

In most misconduct cases, the agency must show that the penalty it chose is reasonable under the twelve Douglas factors — for example, the nature of the offense, the employee's job and disciplinary record, consistency with penalties imposed on similarly situated employees, and the potential for rehabilitation. A deciding official who fails to meaningfully consider these factors frequently loses on penalty, even when the underlying charges are sustained.

Harmful Procedural Error

If the agency violated its own procedures in a way that likely affected the outcome — for example, by failing to give the required advance notice, denying a meaningful opportunity to reply, or relying on materials not disclosed to the employee — the action can be reversed.

Due Process Violations

Federal employees with appeal rights have a constitutionally protected property interest in their positions. Ex parte communications with the deciding official, undisclosed evidence, or reliance on charges outside the proposal notice can each constitute a due process violation warranting reversal without inquiry into harmfulness.

Affirmative Defenses

Discrimination, whistleblower retaliation, and other prohibited personnel practices can also invalidate an adverse action. Where proven, these defenses open the door to make-whole relief and, in some cases, compensatory damages.

Why Retain Counsel Early

The strongest MSPB cases begin before the appeal is filed. Experienced federal employment counsel can preserve the record at the proposal and reply stage, identify and develop affirmative defenses, and negotiate resolutions that avoid hearing risk on both sides.
